Xarius | Unity | Personal

  • Context: solo

Presentation

The third game I created for the arcade machine, Xarius is a game playable by 1 to 4 players locally. Players participate in a space race in ships and must reach the finish line first. I'm particularly proud of this project because of the time it took me to create it: only 5 days to create all the models, music, sounds, code, the two levels and additional content (miniatures, etc.).

Problem

If you've seen the arcade machine at school, you've probably noticed that it only has two joysticks and buttons. So, how did I manage to make a game playable by four players locally on this machine that seems limited to two simultaneous players?

I significantly reduced the controls of each ship by only allowing them to move up and down and left and right. With so few controls, it's possible to assign joysticks to players 1 and 3 and buttons to players 2 and 4. Thus, Xarius became the first game to offer a 4-player mode on this machine!

How does it work?

In the game, each player follows a line that traces the level. They can't move away from it, but can move up and down and left and right along it. The game automatically advances players along this line.

Thanks to these super simple controls, I was able to make a 4-player local experience possible. In-game, players can use speed tunnels to accelerate, but they must also avoid obstacles in their path. At the risk of losing all their speed and having to regain it gradually.

Honestly, there isn't much complexity in this game in terms of code. However, the most technical part remains the level editor.

I developed a script that takes a series of points and traces a level by connecting them. It's possible to add or remove these points, and players automatically follow this path. This allows for quick and efficient level creation. Here are the two levels I created: Earth and Tankerz (a reference to my other game of the same name).

I chose to integrate a map inspired by Tankerz to save time: I already had most of the assets and a musical base. I just had to make some modifications.

The Biggest Challenge

Xarius, 4 players, in space... it's super cool, but try running that on the arcade machine's (desktop) computer! It had to handle 4 cameras and perform separate calculations for each player simultaneously. Result? The poor thing was crying, barely displaying 20 FPS.

What to do? My game was finished, but unplayable due to lack of optimization. So I completely rethought it!

For example, the 3D model of the asteroids contained a huge number of polygons, which was too resource-intensive. I simplified their geometry as well as that of the ships and other game objects. Going from 7,500,000 to 250,000 polygons for 4 players.

But that wasn't enough. I also optimized the materials: removed reflections, metallic effects and shadows, and enabled "GPU Instancing" so all objects share the same material rather than having individual ones.

The result? We go from 20 FPS to a comfortable 60 FPS!

The Music (The Best Part)

Space means grand music. But Jordan Septier means budding composer... or torture for the ears! I had some music skills, but not enough to compose a good soundtrack.

While trying to create the music for Earth, I found a superb bass line and a space piano, but I was stuck. How to continue? I called on a friend, Lauren Lamoureux (thank you a thousand times again!). After four hours on Discord voice chat composing together, I had my best music to date!

For Tankerz, I took the original game's music and remixed it by adapting the instruments. The result made me very proud, especially after applying all of Lauren's advice.

She taught me to create smooth transitions, use an appropriate musical style and choose my instruments well. A huge thank you to her!
