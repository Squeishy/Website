Presentation

HAW, for Home Appliance War, or "The War of Home Appliances", was my very first real game project that seemed like it could lead to something. Although it was never completed, this game taught me a lot: 3D modeling, C#, shaders, animations, online multiplayer, and even account management!

The Beginning of an Adventure

To get to the final (unfinished) game, I had to remake the project... four times! I was very new to Unity at that time, and even after the fourth attempt, it was far from finished. I ended up abandoning it. Because, let's be honest: creating an online multiplayer game at 15, with no experience or idea of how to do it, is clearly not the key to success...

Let's Talk About Multiplayer

For the online gaming system, I used a service called Photon (or Photon.PUN for those in the know), which allowed hosting and playing on online servers. Thanks to this, I learned a lot about how networked games work, including:

  • The distinction between owner and client.

  • The need to synchronize players through a main server (and not a client, at the risk of cheating).

  • The creation of online instances, like projectiles visible to all players.

I discovered the essential basics to avoid cheating issues and ensure a synchronized experience.

You can obviously join online servers and play with people, and change your color too!

Finally, the Home Appliances!

But now, you're probably saying: "I was told this game is called 'The War of Home Appliances', and yet, I don't see any home appliances. I just see a bunch of copies of Kenny McCormick from South Park." Well, that's exactly what we're getting to!

The goal of this game was to push other players into the void with various electrical appliances, for example a toaster or a fridge that sent projectiles to push other players away.

(The image is way too bright but that's Unity's editor bugging, as usual.)

Other appliances used wind to push players, like a vacuum cleaner, a leaf blower, a fan, or even a pressure washer (now that I think about it, I believe Karcher is a registered trademark, should avoid that in the future.)

But that wasn't all! Since the goal of the game was to push each other into the void, there needed to be a way to save yourself. That's how I invented gliders, which allowed you to fly in the sky, for example with a Jet Pack:

The Sound Design

A fun anecdote: for the toaster sound that shoots bread, I placed my toaster and my microphone in a pillowcase to isolate the sound, then recorded while playing with it.

Conclusion

The game was far from perfect and remained quite empty, but it took me a lot of time and taught me so much. At the time, I was fighting against Unity to learn literally everything from scratch. Despite that, it was a super fun project to work on!
